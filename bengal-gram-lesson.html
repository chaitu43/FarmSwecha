Introduction to Bengal Gram

Bengal Gram, or Sanagalu, is the most important pulse crop in India's Rabi season. It is highly valued for its high protein content and is used extensively in various culinary forms. Success in Bengal Gram cultivation depends heavily on soil moisture management and timely sowing.

Soil Moisture Key

Chickpeas require a cool climate and have deep root systems, making them excellent for utilizing sub-soil moisture in black cotton soils during the dry winter months.

Step 1: Ideal Soil Types

Bengal Gram prefers well-drained, heavy soils (black cotton soils or loams). It is sensitive to saline and alkaline conditions.

Detailed Process:

  1. Soil Selection: Choose heavy soils with high water-retention capacity, typical of black cotton zones.
  2. Ploughing: Conduct medium-deep ploughing to ensure a loose seedbed for the crop's deep root system.
  3. Harrowing: Perform 1-2 harrowings to break clods and level the surface.
  4. Nutrient Caution: Avoid excessive nitrogen basal doses to prevent runaway vegetative growth.

Step 2: Sourcing Seeds

Selecting the right variety and high-quality seeds is the first step toward a bountiful rabi harvest.

Detailed Process:

  1. Select Variety: Choose wilt-resistant varieties like JG-11, JAKI-9218, or NBeG-3 (for Andhra Pradesh).
  2. Verify Source: Buy certified seeds from APSSDC, ANGRAU centers, or recognized dealers.
  3. Check Quality: Ensure the seed tag indicates 80%+ germination.
  4. Seed Rate Calculation: Prepare 60–75 kg/ha for small-seeded or 100 kg/ha for bold-seeded varieties.
  5. Pre-Sowing Treatment: treat seeds with Carbendazim (2 g/kg) and Rhizobium culture for disease protection and nitrogen fixation.

Step 3: Sowing Timing

The timing of Bengal Gram sowing depends heavily on the receding monsoon and soil temperature.

Detailed Process:

  1. Optimal Sowing Window: Plant between mid-October and mid-November for best results in Andhra Pradesh.
  2. Sowing Depth: Plant seeds deep (8-10 cm) to utilize residual sub-soil moisture.
  3. Spacing Standard: Maintain 30 cm between rows and 10 cm between plants.
  4. Thinning Checklist: Ensure uniform plant spacing after emergence to avoid overcrowding.
  5. Moisture Management: Bengal Gram usually grows on residual moisture; provide one light irrigation at pre-flowering if the soil cracks significantly.

Step 4: Harvesting

Timely harvesting prevents grain loss due to over-drying and pod shattering.

Detailed Process:

  1. Maturity Check: Harvest when leaves turn yellow-brown and pods are fully dry/brittle.
  2. Collection Method: Cut the plants manually using a sickle or pull them out if the soil is loose.
  3. Solar Drying: Spread harvested plants on the threshing floor for 2-3 days under direct sunlight.
  4. Threshing: Beat with sticks or use a mechanical thresher to separate the chickpeas from the pods.
  5. Winnowing: Clean the grains of any chaff, stones, or dust.
  6. Final Drying: Dry the grains until the moisture reaches 10-12% for safe storage.
  7. Storage Best Practices: Store in airtight bins or bags to prevent damage from pulse beetles.

Pro Tip: Wilt Management

Fusarium Wilt is a devastating disease. Practice crop rotation with cereals and use wilt-resistant varieties like JG-11 or JAKI-9218 to safeguard your harvest.
